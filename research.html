Research

Working Paper:

  1.  Network of Loyalty Programs: A Sequential Formation (submitted)

    Abstract: Abstract By forming partnerships with hotel chains and airlines, major credit card issuers in the U.S. allow customers to transfer points from their loyalty programs to the partners'. Hotel chains form similar partnerships with airlines, and airlines with other airlines. Viewing their partnerships as a network and using a sequential network formation model, this article studies how credit card issuers and hotel chains strategically choose to add, delete, or maintain partnerships with airlines. Using a novel dataset that involves 3 credit card issuers, 7 hotel chains, and 43 airlines from 2014 to 2018, the estimation result suggests that a credit card issuer is more likely to form a partnership with an airline that (1) is a partner of another credit card issuer (2) is a partner of its hotel chain partner (3) better complements its existing portfolio of airline partners, after accounting for key characteristics of firms. The first two results are similar when hotel chains choose airline partners.

Work in Progress:

  1.  Search Cost and Information on Price Discounts: Your Consumer Surplus May Vary

    Abstract: Online social shopping platforms enable ordinary shoppers to share information on price discounts, product quality, and their opinions on whether it is a "good deal". Such practices not only reduce search costs for the users but also allow them to make purchases at lower prices. Moreover, the shopping platforms form partnerships with manufacturers and retailers to allow price discounts exclusive for their users. This paper studies how an online social shopping platform creates discrepancies in prices faced by online shoppers and their implications on consumer welfare. I focus on price discrepancies in identical electronics products sold by reputable online retailers. For a large number of electronics products, I utilize spot prices on Amazon.com (serve as "standard" online prices), spot prices from postings on the shopping platform ("discount" online prices), and Clickstream data (for purchase verification on the shopping platform) to study two market outcomes. First, I check how the price discrepancies, in percentage terms, depend on product characteristics, such as list prices. Second, I quantify the discrepancy in consumer welfare induced by the shopping platform.
